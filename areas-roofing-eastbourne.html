Roofers in Eastbourne

Eastbourne is our home town and the base from which M.W. Pyle Roofing has operated for over 35 years. We know Eastbourne's housing stock, its streets, and its weather better than any contractor passing through.

Roofing in Eastbourne — what you need to know

Eastbourne has an incredibly varied housing stock. You'll find Victorian and Edwardian terraces in the town centre and Old Town, substantial detached villas in Meads, 1930s semi-detached houses across Hampden Park and Willingdon, post-war estates in Langney and Sovereign Harbour, and modern new-builds on the town's edges. Each property type comes with its own roofing characteristics — and its own common problems.

Being a coastal town, Eastbourne roofs face salt air corrosion that simply doesn't occur a few miles inland. This accelerates the rusting of fixing nails, the cracking of ridge mortar, and the deterioration of lead flashings. Properties closest to the seafront — particularly in Meads, the Wish area, and along the seafront itself — see the worst of this. But even properties in Hampden Park and Langney experience meaningfully higher corrosion rates than they would in, say, Hailsham or Uckfield.

The most common roofing problems we see in Eastbourne

Slipped and nail-sick slates on Victorian and Edwardian properties. Eastbourne has thousands of original Victorian and Edwardian slate roofs, and the vast majority are now at the stage where the original iron fixing nails have corroded through — a condition called "nail sickness." The slates themselves may still be in perfectly good condition, but once the nails fail, slates begin sliding down the roof. This is not something you can fix by replacing individual tiles; the entire roof needs to be stripped, re-nailed with stainless steel fixings, and re-laid. We carry out this work regularly across the Old Town, Meads, and the terraced streets of central Eastbourne.

Failed ridge mortar. The mortar bedding that holds ridge tiles in place has a lifespan of around 20–30 years. In Eastbourne's coastal climate, exposed ridge lines often fail sooner. We see ridge tiles that have lifted, cracked, or come loose entirely on properties all over Eastbourne — it's one of our most common call-outs. Left unattended, loose ridge tiles are a storm hazard and allow water to penetrate the ridge line.

Flat roof failures on extensions and garages. Eastbourne has a large stock of post-war and 1970s–80s housing with felt flat roofs on rear extensions and garages. Most of these are now well past their lifespan and leaking. We replace them regularly — and we always recommend moving away from traditional felt when you do.

Tiles vs slates for Eastbourne properties — which is right for your home?

Natural Welsh slate

For Victorian and Edwardian properties in Eastbourne, natural Welsh slate is almost always the correct material — and in conservation areas like the Old Town, it may be required by the council. Natural slate has a lifespan of 75–100 years and handles coastal salt air better than any alternative. It's the most expensive option upfront, but the mathematics over a lifetime almost always favour it. A properly installed natural slate roof on an Eastbourne Victorian terrace should outlast the building's next two sets of owners.

Synthetic (fibre cement) slate

A very good alternative to natural slate where budget is a constraint or the property isn't in a conservation area. Modern synthetic slates have improved dramatically — the better products carry 40–50 year warranties and are virtually indistinguishable from natural slate in appearance. For 1930s and post-war properties in Hampden Park, Langney and Willingdon, synthetic slate is often the most sensible choice.

Concrete interlocking tiles

Common on 1960s–90s housing across Eastbourne's outer estates. They're functional and affordable to replace like-for-like, but they have a lifespan of only 25–30 years and perform relatively poorly in coastal salt air compared to slate or clay. If your Eastbourne home has concrete tiles that are approaching the end of their life, it's worth considering an upgrade to clay or synthetic slate at re-roofing time.

Clay plain tiles

Where concrete tiles are installed on older Eastbourne properties that would originally have had clay, upgrading back to clay at re-roofing time is a worthwhile investment. Clay tiles have a lifespan of 60+ years, handle coastal exposure better than concrete, and look significantly better on period properties. The cost difference over a 60-year lifespan is negligible.

Flat roofs in Eastbourne — felt, liquid plastic or single ply?

Traditional felt (not recommended)

We're honest with our customers: we don't recommend traditional three-layer felt flat roofing for Eastbourne properties. Its lifespan is 10–15 years at best — often less on south-facing or exposed roofs. The seams are vulnerable, UV degradation is rapid, and once it starts to fail it usually fails quickly. The only situation where felt makes sense is if you're on an extremely tight budget and need a temporary solution for a roof you plan to demolish or radically alter in the next few years.

GRP fibreglass (liquid plastic)

This is our most commonly recommended flat roof solution for Eastbourne extensions and garages. A GRP (glass reinforced plastic) fibreglass roof is laid as a seamless, joint-free surface with no weak points for water to penetrate. It has a lifespan of 25–30 years, is extremely low maintenance, handles UV exposure and temperature fluctuations well, and can be walked on without damage. It costs more than felt upfront — typically 40–60% more — but you'll likely never need to replace it again.

Single ply membrane

For larger flat roof areas — extensions over 30–40 square metres, flat-roofed bungalows, or commercial flat roofs — single ply membrane systems are worth serious consideration. They're extremely durable (40+ year lifespan with proper maintenance), perform well in coastal conditions, and come with manufacturer-backed warranties. The installation cost is higher than GRP but the long-term economics are excellent for larger roof areas.

Chimney repairs and removal in Eastbourne

Eastbourne's Victorian and Edwardian housing stock is full of chimney stacks — many of which haven't had a fire lit beneath them for 30 or 40 years. These redundant chimneys cause a surprising number of problems: failing mortar lets water in; cracked or leaning pots become a hazard; and the lead flashings where the stack meets the roof are a very common source of leaks.

Repointing and flashing repair — if the stack itself is structurally sound, repointing the brickwork and renewing the lead flashing is often all that's needed. We do this regularly across Eastbourne.

Chimney pot replacement — cracked or missing chimney pots allow rainwater straight into the flue. If you're keeping the chimney, the pots should be sound. If not, the flue should be capped and vented.

Full chimney removal — for genuinely redundant stacks that are causing maintenance issues, removing the chimney entirely (below the roof line, with the roof made good) is often the most cost-effective long-term solution. It eliminates a permanent maintenance liability and we make the roof completely weathertight. This is a job we carry out frequently in Eastbourne and is worth serious consideration if your chimney is causing repeated problems.

Note: if your property is in a conservation area or is listed, you may need planning permission to remove or significantly alter a chimney. We can advise on this as part of a free inspection.

Questions & answers — Eastbourne roofing

My slates keep slipping — do I need a full re-roof?

Possibly, but not necessarily. If only a handful of slates have slipped and the roof is relatively young, individual repairs may be sufficient. But if your Eastbourne Victorian or Edwardian property has original slates and they're slipping widely across the roof, the nails have almost certainly corroded through and a full strip and re-lay is the only lasting solution. We can inspect and tell you honestly which situation you're in.

How long does a flat roof last in Eastbourne?

Traditional felt: 10–15 years. GRP fibreglass: 25–30 years. Single ply membrane: 40+ years with maintenance. Given Eastbourne's coastal UV exposure, we always recommend GRP or single ply over felt — the longer lifespan more than justifies the small additional upfront cost.

Do I need planning permission to replace my roof in Eastbourne?

Replacing like-for-like doesn't usually require planning permission. However, if you're in the Old Town conservation area, or if your property is listed, there may be restrictions on materials. We can advise on this during a free inspection.

Can you match the existing slate on my Victorian terrace?

Yes. We source natural Welsh slate that closely matches the original material used on Eastbourne's Victorian stock. For properties where an exact match is important — especially in terraces where the roofline is visible together — we take care to specify the right slate.
