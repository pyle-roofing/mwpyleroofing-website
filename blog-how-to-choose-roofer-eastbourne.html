How to Choose a Reliable Roofer in East Sussex — A Complete Guide

Choosing a roofer is one of the bigger decisions you'll make as a homeowner. Get it wrong and you could spend thousands on poor work that fails quickly, or hand money to someone who disappears without completing the job. Here is everything you need to know to make the right choice in Eastbourne, Hastings, Bexhill, Lewes, Brighton or anywhere in East Sussex.

Roofing is one of the least regulated trades in the UK. There is no statutory licensing requirement to call yourself a roofer — anyone can buy a van, print some business cards, and start knocking on doors. The result is that the quality of roofing work available in East Sussex varies enormously, from genuinely excellent craftsmen with decades of local experience and deep knowledge of the area's specific conditions, to fly-by-night operators whose work fails within months.

After 40 years of trading in Eastbourne and across East Sussex, we've seen — and been called to fix — a very large amount of poor roofing work. We understand both sides of this: we know what excellent roofing work looks like, and we know exactly how bad roofing work comes about and what to look for to avoid it. This is our honest guide to choosing the right roofer — for any job, of any size, anywhere in East Sussex.

Why this decision matters more than you might think

It's tempting to approach roofing work the same way you'd approach choosing a plumber for a minor repair — find someone who seems reasonable, get a quote, get it done. For small repairs, this is probably fine. For anything significant — a full or partial re-roof, a flat roof replacement, chimney rebuilding, or extensive leadwork — the stakes are considerably higher.

Poor roofing work can fail quickly and expensively. We regularly inspect roofs in Eastbourne, Bexhill, Hastings and across East Sussex where previous work has failed within 2–5 years — work that should have lasted 20–30 years with any level of competence and appropriate materials. The homeowners involved have typically paid a second contractor to fix the original problem correctly, having already spent money on work that made no lasting difference.

There is also a safety dimension. A poorly fixed ridge tile in Eastbourne or Polegate's exposed position is not just a maintenance problem — it is a falling hazard. Incorrectly installed lead flashings on a Brighton Regency terrace can allow water into a property for years before the source is identified, causing structural timber damage and significant interior harm. A chimney stack that is repointed incorrectly deteriorates faster than it would have without the intervention.

Taking the time to choose the right roofer protects your investment, your home, and in some cases, the safety of people below your roof.

What to look for in a reliable East Sussex roofer

1. Verifiable local presence and long trading history

A roofing company that has been trading in East Sussex for many years is a fundamentally different proposition from one that appeared recently with a smart website and no local track record. A long-established company has a reputation to protect — not just with individual customers, but with neighbours, local builders' merchants, and the broader community. They also have the accumulated knowledge of local housing stock, local planning requirements (important in Lewes and Hastings conservation areas), and the specific challenges of the East Sussex coastal environment.

Verifying local presence is straightforward: search the company name on Companies House to see when they were incorporated and who the directors are. Search their name alongside East Sussex location names to see what comes up over an extended time period. Ask how long they've been trading — a genuine established company will tell you readily and specifically. M.W. Pyle Roofing has been trading in Eastbourne for over 40 years. We're easy to verify and we're not going anywhere.

2. Public liability insurance — ask for the certificate

Any reputable roofing contractor in East Sussex must carry public liability insurance. This protects you — the homeowner — if a roofer damages your property or injures a third party while working on your home. Without it, any damage caused during roofing work could become your problem to pursue through civil litigation against an individual, which is a lengthy and often fruitless process.

Don't just ask whether they have insurance — ask to see the certificate before any work begins. It should clearly state the level of cover (a minimum of £1 million for public liability is standard; many reputable contractors carry £2 million or more), the policy period (check it hasn't expired), and the name of the insured party (which should match the company or individual doing the work). We are fully insured and provide evidence of this as a matter of course.

3. Written, itemised quotations — not verbal or lump-sum prices

A professional roofing contractor provides a written quotation that clearly specifies what work will be carried out, what materials will be used (by type and where possible by manufacturer and specification), what is included in the price and what is excluded, the anticipated start date and duration, and the payment terms. This is not bureaucracy — it is the minimum level of clarity you need to understand what you're agreeing to and to have any meaningful basis for comparison between contractors.

A quote that says "re-roof house — £7,500" tells you almost nothing. What tiles? What batten spacing? Is new felt included? What happens to the waste? Is the scaffold included? What's the position on VAT? A quote that says "strip existing concrete interlocking tiles, replace sarking felt with breathable membrane, new treated timber battens at 300mm centres, re-roof in Marley Anglia concrete interlocking tiles, lead flashings to chimney renewed, all waste removed — £7,500 including scaffold and VAT" tells you exactly what you're getting.

If a contractor is unwilling or unable to provide a written itemised quotation, that is a significant warning sign. They may be planning to add items later that weren't in the original verbal price, or they may not have the knowledge to specify the job correctly.

4. Two or three quotes — but understand what you're comparing

Getting two or three quotes for significant roofing work is good practice. However, comparing quotes is only meaningful if they are for the same scope and specification. A quote that is £1,500 cheaper than another may be cheaper because it specifies inferior tiles, skips the felt replacement, or excludes scaffold. A quote that is more expensive may be so because it includes items the cheaper quote simply hasn't mentioned.

When you receive multiple quotes, read them carefully and note any differences in scope. If something is included in one and not another, ask both contractors about it. A genuine professional will explain the reasoning for their specification; someone trying to win on price by cutting scope will often become evasive when questioned.

Be particularly cautious of a quote that is dramatically cheaper than all others. In roofing, as in most trades, the market price for a given job is what it costs to do it properly with appropriate materials and skilled labour. A price that is substantially below market rate is usually below market rate for a reason — inferior materials, inexperienced labour, scope that will expand once the job is open, or a contractor who simply doesn't intend to complete the work.

5. Do not pay large deposits upfront

This is perhaps the most important practical piece of advice in this guide. Rogue traders operating in East Sussex after storms and otherwise typically request large cash deposits upfront — often 50% or more of the total job cost. They then disappear, or complete a fraction of the work and demand further payment before continuing.

A reputable, well-established roofing company does not need large upfront deposits. They have relationships with suppliers who extend credit, they have cash flow from ongoing work, and they have a business model that doesn't depend on your deposit to fund the materials for your job. A small deposit to cover the first material delivery on a large job is reasonable — typically 10–15% of the job value. A request for 50% or more upfront is a red flag regardless of how professional the company appears in other respects.

For larger jobs, a staged payment structure tied to defined stages of completion is entirely appropriate — for example, 30% at the start, 50% on completion of the main roof covering, 20% on final completion and satisfaction sign-off. Payments should always be made on completion of each stage rather than in advance of it.

6. Check reviews carefully — and how the company responds to them

Google reviews, Checkatrade and Which? Trusted Traders are all worth consulting. Look at several things beyond the overall star rating: how recent are the reviews (a company with a good historical rating that has no recent reviews may have changed in quality); how does the company respond to any negative reviews (professionally and with a genuine attempt to resolve the issue is the right response); are the reviews specific and credible (genuine reviews typically describe the specific work done and specific aspects of the service, not just general praise).

Personal recommendations from people you know in East Sussex — neighbours, friends, family members who have had roofing work done — are generally the most reliable form of vetting. If three people on your street have used the same company and all rate them highly, that is a meaningful signal.

7. Questions to ask before agreeing any work

Before signing a contract or agreeing to proceed with any significant roofing work in East Sussex, these are the questions worth asking:

  • How long have you been trading, and can I verify this?
  • Can I see your public liability insurance certificate?
  • Can you provide references from similar jobs completed recently in this area?
  • What specifically are you proposing to use — tile manufacturer and product, felt specification, fixing type and gauge?
  • Is scaffold included in this quote?
  • Who will actually be doing the work — your own employed team, or subcontractors?
  • What is the payment schedule?
  • What warranty or guarantee do you offer on the work?
  • What is the process if additional work is identified once the roof is stripped?

A reputable contractor answers all of these readily and specifically. Evasion on any of them warrants caution.

What to expect during roofing work — and after

Knowing what a professional roofing job looks like helps you identify if something isn't right during the work.

Before work starts: You should receive written confirmation of the start date, a clear explanation of access requirements (scaffold erection, parking needs for a skip or tile delivery), and contact details for the person responsible for the job.

During the work: The roof should be weathertight at the end of every working day — no open sections left unprotected overnight. Materials should arrive in appropriate quantities for the job. The team should be working systematically and obviously progressing. If you have questions, a professional team welcomes them.

If additional issues are discovered: When a roof is stripped, it sometimes reveals problems that weren't visible before — rotten timbers, failed felt that wasn't visible from outside, structural issues. A professional contractor stops work, shows you or photographs the issue, explains what additional work is needed and what it costs, and waits for your agreement before proceeding. They do not carry out additional work without your consent and then present an inflated bill on completion.

On completion: All waste materials should be removed from the property. Gutters and surrounding areas should be clean. You should be offered a walk-around inspection. The contractor should be willing to return to address any snags identified in the days following completion.

Red flags — what to watch for at every stage

  • Cold calling at the door claiming to have "noticed" damage. We never do this. Genuine roofing companies rely on recommendations and established relationships, not door-knocking after storms.
  • Pressure to decide immediately — "we're in the area today only" or "we have materials left over from a nearby job." This artificial urgency is a classic tactic.
  • Cash only. Legitimate businesses accept bank transfer and issue receipts. Cash-only requests make disputes over what was agreed almost impossible to resolve.
  • No written quote. If it's not in writing, it doesn't exist.
  • Reluctance to provide insurance or reference details. This is your home — you are entirely within your rights to ask for these before agreeing to anything.
  • Substandard materials visible on site. If you can see that the materials being used look thin, cheap or inconsistent with what was specified, ask questions.
  • Aggressive behaviour if you question anything. A professional contractor welcomes questions. Someone with something to hide does not.

Guarantees and warranties — what to expect

Reputable roofing contractors stand behind their work. A guarantee on workmanship for 5–10 years is reasonable for a full re-roof. Material warranties are provided by the tile manufacturer separately — most major concrete tile manufacturers provide 30-year warranties; quality clay tile manufacturers often provide 50-year warranties; Welsh slate carries lifetime natural material warranties. Ask your contractor for copies of any manufacturer documentation.

Note that workmanship guarantees from small businesses are only as good as the business's continued existence — they're not underwritten in the way that warranty insurance products are. A company that has been trading in East Sussex for 40 years represents a more meaningful guarantee than one that has been trading for two.

Questions & answers

How do I check whether a roofer is legitimate?

Search their company name on Companies House — this tells you when the company was registered, who the directors are, and the company's financial filing history. Search their name alongside "East Sussex" in Google and look at what appears over time. Call their landline number (not just a mobile). Ask for the address of their business premises. Ask for the names of recent customers who would speak to their work. All of these take minutes and collectively tell you a great deal.

The cheapest quote is £2,000 less than the others — should I take it?

First, establish whether it is for the same scope and specification as the other quotes. If one quote specifies breathable felt and stainless steel fixings and another specifies standard felt and galvanised fixings, they are not comparable. If the specification appears similar and the price difference is still substantial, ask the cheapest contractor specifically what accounts for the lower price. If you can't get a clear answer, the lower price is either based on a lower quality of work, an intent to add costs later, or a risk to the business that means the guarantee is worth little.

Can I get a grant or financial assistance for roofing work?

In some limited circumstances, yes. If you are on certain benefits or if your home has a low energy performance rating, you may be eligible for assistance through government energy efficiency schemes — some of which cover roof insulation as part of a broader package. Your local council (Eastbourne Borough Council, Wealden District Council, Lewes District Council etc) may also have local improvement grant schemes for certain property types. It is worth checking, though the availability and eligibility criteria change regularly.

What happens if work is done and I'm not satisfied?

First, raise the issue directly with the contractor in writing — email is fine — describing clearly what you are not satisfied with. Most genuine disputes are resolved at this stage. If the contractor disputes your concern, ask them to inspect the specific issue with you present. If you cannot reach agreement, Trading Standards (accessible through the Citizens Advice Bureau) can advise on your rights and the process for escalating a dispute. This is why written quotations and written agreements matter — disputes are far easier to resolve when both parties' obligations are clearly documented.
