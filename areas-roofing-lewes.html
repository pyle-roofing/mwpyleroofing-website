Roofers in Lewes, East Sussex

Lewes is one of East Sussex's most historic towns, with a housing stock unlike anywhere else in the region — flint buildings, Horsham stone roofs, listed properties and a conservation area that runs through the heart of the town. We have extensive experience working with traditional materials here.

Roofing in Lewes — a town with unique demands

Lewes demands a level of care and knowledge that many roofing contractors simply don't have. The town centre and large parts of the surrounding streets fall within the Lewes conservation area, and the council takes the appearance and material specification of roofing seriously. Many properties in Lewes are also listed — some at Grade I and II* — where even minor roofing work requires careful consideration of materials and method.

The housing stock spans an extraordinary range. Medieval timber-framed buildings in the High Street with clay plain tile roofs. Georgian townhouses with Horsham stone or Welsh slate. Victorian terraces in the streets off School Hill. Edwardian and inter-war properties in the residential streets to the north and south. And a significant stock of post-war and modern housing further out towards Landport and South Malling. We work across all of them.

The most common roofing problems in Lewes

Horsham stone roofing. Some of the older properties in and around Lewes — particularly farmhouses, cottages and barns on the edges of town — have original Horsham stone roofs. Horsham stone is a local sandstone that was used as a roofing material for centuries and is now extremely rare and expensive. When Horsham stone roofs need repair, sourcing matching stone is the central challenge. We work carefully with reclaimed stone and take great care not to damage original material that may be irreplaceable.

Failed lead flashings on Georgian properties. Lewes has a significant number of handsome Georgian buildings — many of them now converted to offices, shops or flats — with original or early replacement lead flashings around parapets, chimney stacks and mansard sections. When these fail, the water ingress can be significant and damage valuable interiors. Lead repair and replacement on Georgian buildings in the Lewes conservation area is work we approach with particular care.

Moss on north-facing slopes. Lewes sits in the Ouse valley and some parts of the town are relatively sheltered and damp — ideal conditions for moss. Many properties in the streets north of the High Street have significant moss growth on their north-facing roof slopes.

Roofing materials in Lewes — what's appropriate and what lasts

Natural slate

Natural Welsh slate is the correct and most frequently specified material for re-roofing in the Lewes conservation area and for listed properties. It has a lifespan of 75–100 years and weathers beautifully. Lewes District Council conservation officers generally expect like-for-like natural slate replacement for properties within the conservation area — synthetic alternatives are often not approved.

Clay plain tiles

Clay plain tiles are the correct material for many of Lewes's Victorian and Edwardian properties, and for cottages and farmhouses throughout the surrounding villages. They last 60+ years, look right on period buildings, and are generally accepted in the conservation area. We source appropriate profiles — the handmade appearance of good clay plain tiles is particularly appropriate for older Lewes properties.

Concrete tiles — and when to upgrade

Some post-war and 1960s–80s properties in Landport and South Malling have concrete interlocking tiles. These are functional and appropriate for those property types, but when they reach the end of their life — typically 25–30 years — it's worth considering whether clay or synthetic slate might be a better long-term choice. Lewes is wetter than the coastal towns but without the salt air, so concrete tiles don't face the same corrosion pressures — but their shorter lifespan is still a consideration.

Flat roofs in Lewes — GRP is the clear choice

Extensions, garages and flat-roofed sections are common across Lewes's varied housing stock. Given Lewes's reasonable rainfall levels and the fact that the Ouse valley can be damp, a seamless system is particularly important. GRP fibreglass flat roofing — with its 25–30 year lifespan and zero joints — is our strong recommendation over felt for any flat roof replacement in Lewes. For larger flat sections, single ply is worth discussing.

Chimney work in Lewes — when to repoint, rebuild or remove

Lewes's historic chimneys are a major part of the town's roofscape and the conservation area designation reflects how seriously the council takes their preservation. Chimney removal below roof level is unlikely to be approved within the conservation area, and for listed properties any significant chimney work requires listed building consent.

Within the conservation area, we typically carry out repointing and pot replacement on redundant stacks — keeping the structure sound and weathertight while maintaining its visual contribution to the townscape. For stacks that have deteriorated beyond repointing, chimney rebuilding in matching brick is the appropriate solution. We work carefully to source matching brick and mortar profiles for conservation and listed property work in Lewes.

For properties outside the conservation area, full removal of a genuinely redundant chimney is a reasonable option when the stack is causing persistent maintenance problems — and we carry this out regularly on the post-war and modern housing stock in Landport and South Malling.

Roof cleaning in Lewes — the right approach for historic roofs

Jet washing is never appropriate on the roofing materials found in Lewes — it damages clay plain tiles, strips natural slate surfaces, and can physically dislodge the smaller roof coverings found on some historic properties. We never use jet washing. Our approach is manual removal of growth using soft brushes, followed by a professional biocidal treatment that kills moss, lichen and algae at root level.

For properties in the Lewes conservation area and for listed buildings, we take particular care with biocidal treatments — ensuring the product is appropriate for the material and won't cause staining or surface damage to valuable historic fabric.

Copper ridge strips are highly effective in Lewes's damp valley climate. Properties we've cleaned and fitted copper strips to are staying significantly cleaner for longer than those treated without — the copper ions washed down each rainfall simply prevent moss re-establishing.

Preventing damp in Lewes homes

Lewes's position in the Ouse valley means some properties — particularly those on lower ground — can be damp from multiple sources. When investigating damp in a Lewes property it's important to distinguish between roof-related ingress, wall penetration through flint or render, rising damp at ground level, and condensation. A proper diagnosis before spending money on repairs is essential.

Roof-related damp prevention in Lewes: keep gutters clear and flowing freely, ensure downpipes discharge away from the building, maintain lead flashings before they fail, and address moss growth before it lifts tiles and allows water in. An annual check of the roof from inside the loft on a bright day — looking for daylight or staining on timbers — costs nothing and catches problems early.

Questions & answers — roofing in Lewes

My property is in the Lewes conservation area — do I need permission to re-roof?

Re-roofing like-for-like generally doesn't require planning permission, even in the conservation area — but changing materials (from slate to concrete tile, for example) would require consent. If your property is listed, any roofing work should be discussed with Lewes District Council before proceeding. We can advise on what's likely to be acceptable and have experience working within conservation area requirements.

I have a Horsham stone roof — can you repair it?

Yes, though it requires careful sourcing of matching stone. We work with Horsham stone and have access to reclaimed material. The priority is always to preserve as much original stone as possible and replace only what is broken or missing. Please call us for a specific assessment — Horsham stone work is priced individually.

Is the damp in my Lewes home coming from the roof?

Not necessarily. Lewes properties can be damp from several sources — roof leaks, penetrating damp through flint walls, rising damp and condensation are all common. We'll inspect the roof and give you an honest view on whether the roof is the source. If it's something else, we'll tell you that too rather than selling you unnecessary work.

How long does natural slate last in Lewes?

A properly installed natural Welsh slate roof in Lewes — away from the salt air of the coast — should last 75–100 years. The slates themselves are almost indefinitely durable; what fails is the iron nails used in Victorian and Edwardian installations. Re-nailing with stainless steel fixings at the point of re-lay means the slates should outlast most of the people who will ever own the property.
