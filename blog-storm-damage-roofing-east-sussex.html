Storm Damage Roof Repairs in East Sussex — A Complete Guide

East Sussex gets hit hard by Channel storms. Whether you're in coastal Eastbourne, Bexhill or Hastings, or inland in Hailsham, Lewes or Polegate, a bad storm can leave your roof damaged and your home exposed. Here's exactly what to do — step by step.

Living in East Sussex means living with Channel storms. The stretch of coastline from Brighton and Hove through Eastbourne, Bexhill and Hastings to St Leonards-on-Sea is one of the most storm-exposed in England, regularly battered by southwesterly gales that build over hundreds of miles of open water before hitting the coast with considerable force. Inland, towns like Hailsham, Polegate, Lewes and Pevensey aren't sheltered enough to avoid serious storm damage — particularly in autumn and winter when pressure systems track across southern England in quick succession.

In that time, we've learned not just how to fix it — but the right order to do things, how to work with insurers, and how to protect yourself from the rogue traders who appear in force after every major weather event. This guide covers all of it.

Why East Sussex roofs are particularly vulnerable to storm damage

East Sussex roofs face a specific combination of factors that makes storm damage more common here than in many other parts of England.

Wind exposure. The Channel coast from Brighton to Hastings faces almost directly into the southwest — the direction of Britain's prevailing wind and most of its major storm tracks. There is no land mass of significance between East Sussex and the Atlantic to absorb wind energy. Properties on the seafront in Eastbourne, along the coast at Bexhill and Hastings, and on elevated ground like the ridge above Hastings or the South Downs edge above Eastbourne and Polegate, face some of the highest domestic wind loads in southern England.

Salt air weakening. Decades of salt air exposure corrodes the metal components that hold roofs together — fixing nails, screws, brackets and clips. A roof that looked perfectly sound in dry summer conditions can prove vulnerable in a winter storm because the nails holding down ridge tiles or the brackets securing guttering have been slowly eaten away by salt corrosion over many years. This is particularly acute on seafront properties in Eastbourne, Pevensey Bay, Bexhill and Hastings — but it extends further inland than most homeowners realise.

Ageing housing stock. A large proportion of East Sussex's housing was built between 1880 and 1940. These properties are now 80–140 years old, and their roofing systems — even those that have been partially repaired over the years — are working with original or aging components that are more susceptible to storm forces than modern installations.

What to do immediately after a storm — step by step

Step 1: Stay safe and stay off the roof

This cannot be overstated. A wet roof immediately after a storm is one of the most dangerous surfaces you can stand on. Moss-covered tiles are as slippery as ice. Tiles damaged by the storm may be loose, cracked or about to slide. There is absolutely nothing you can usefully do on the roof that justifies the risk of a serious fall. Do not go up there under any circumstances.

Equally, be cautious near the base of walls after a storm if you have ridge tiles, chimney pots or sections of guttering that may have fallen or may be loose above. Keep a safe distance from anything that could still be coming down.

Step 2: Assess from the ground and from inside the loft

From the ground — ideally with binoculars — look at the roof carefully. Note any obviously missing ridge tiles, displaced slates or tiles, damaged chimney stacks, or sections of guttering that have fallen or are hanging loose. Take photographs of everything you can see — this documentation is important for your insurance claim.

Go into the loft with a torch. Look for any daylight visible through the roof covering — this indicates a gap where a tile or slate is missing. Look for any water dripping or staining on the timbers or the felt. Note where and how extensive any damage appears. Photograph what you find.

Step 3: Control interior water damage

If water is entering the property, act quickly to minimise the damage inside. Move furniture and valuables away from affected areas. Lay down towels, use buckets and large containers, and protect floor coverings. Photograph all interior damage before moving anything where possible — again, this is evidence for your insurance claim.

If water is pooling in a ceiling and the plasterboard looks like it may give way — visible as a bulging, darkening patch that feels soft when pressed carefully — it is sometimes better to pierce the ceiling at the lowest point with a screwdriver or knife to allow the water to drain in a controlled way. A ceiling that collapses suddenly and unexpectedly causes significantly more damage to the room below than a controlled drainage point.

Step 4: Contact your insurer

Notify your buildings insurer as soon as possible — most policies require prompt notification of damage. You don't need to have all the details of the repair cost at this stage; just report that storm damage has occurred and that you are taking steps to prevent further damage and arranging for a professional assessment. Ask your insurer whether they need to appoint their own loss adjuster before repair work begins, or whether you can proceed with repairs using your own contractor.

Many insurers are happy for you to proceed with emergency weatherproofing and then agree the full repair scope — they would rather you prevented further damage than waited weeks for their own surveyor to visit. But get this confirmed in writing (or at least noted with a reference number from your call) before spending significant money on repairs.

The most common types of storm damage we repair in East Sussex

Blown-off or displaced ridge tiles

Ridge tiles are the most common single item of storm damage across East Sussex. They sit at the very peak of the roof, exposed to maximum wind force, held in place by mortar that deteriorates over 20–30 years. Once the mortar cracks, wind pressure beneath a slightly lifted ridge tile rapidly removes it entirely. We repair and replace ridge tiles across East Sussex constantly — and where the mortar has failed on multiple tiles, we recommend mechanically fixing the new ridge tiles with stainless steel screws in addition to re-bedding in mortar, particularly on exposed properties in Polegate, Willingdon, Pevensey Bay and Eastbourne's south-facing slopes.

Missing and slipped slates and tiles

Tiles and slates that were already nail-sick — held in place by a corroded nail with little remaining strength — are frequently dislodged entirely by storm force winds. In some cases, the storm reveals widespread nail sickness that wasn't causing problems until the additional wind load was applied. Individual tiles can often be replaced straightforwardly. Widespread displacement across a large area of roof usually means a strip and re-lay is the appropriate solution — the storm has simply accelerated what was going to happen anyway.

Lead flashing damage

Lead flashings around chimney stacks, dormer windows, skylights and abutments can be lifted, cracked or pulled away from their fixings by storm-force wind-driven rain. A failed flashing can allow large quantities of water into a roof void in a short storm period — much more than a missing individual tile. Flashing repairs are specialist work and need to be done correctly; poorly repaired flashings are a recurring source of leaks in East Sussex properties that have had storm repair work carried out by less experienced contractors.

Guttering, fascia and soffit damage

Heavy debris loads in guttering combined with storm wind forces frequently tear guttering sections from their brackets, break downpipe joints, and in some cases take sections of fascia board with them. Guttering damage is straightforward to repair but should be done promptly — a broken section of guttering in East Sussex's wet climate will deposit significant amounts of water down the wall below it every time it rains.

Chimney damage

Chimney stacks — particularly older ones in poor condition — are vulnerable to storm damage in two ways. First, loose or poorly mortared chimney pots can blow off entirely, falling dangerously and creating an open flue through which rain can pour. Second, already-weakened brickwork can be displaced or in extreme cases toppled. A chimney pot or section of brickwork falling through a roof causes severe structural damage. If your chimney looks damaged or unstable after a storm, do not re-enter the property through the rooms directly beneath it without professional assessment.

Flat roof membrane damage

Storm-force wind creates uplift pressure on flat roofs — an upward lifting force that can peel back poorly bonded or already-deteriorated flat roof coverings. Felt flat roofs are particularly vulnerable to this as the laps and edges can be caught by the wind. Single ply and GRP membranes that are fully bonded resist storm uplift significantly better. If your extension or garage flat roof has been disturbed by a storm, it needs prompt assessment — a partially lifted membrane will allow rapid water ingress.

How to work with your insurer on storm damage repairs

Most standard home buildings insurance policies cover storm damage to roofs, and the process of claiming is generally more straightforward than homeowners expect — provided the damage is clearly storm-related rather than the result of gradual wear and tear. Here is how to approach it effectively.

Document everything before repair work starts. Photographs taken before any repair work is done are your most valuable evidence. Photo-document the external damage from the ground, any internal water damage, and anything in the loft. Note the date and time of photographs where possible — most smartphones do this automatically in the file metadata.

Get a written assessment from your roofer. A detailed written report from a roofing contractor describing the storm damage, its cause, and the repair required is extremely useful for insurance purposes. We provide this as a matter of course — ask us specifically for a written storm damage assessment when we attend, and we'll ensure it clearly attributes the damage to the storm event rather than pre-existing issues.

Don't over-claim or under-claim. Your insurer will cover the repair of storm damage — they won't cover the replacement of a roof that was already at the end of its life before the storm. If the storm caused £800 of ridge tile damage on an otherwise sound roof, claim for the £800. Don't try to use the storm claim to fund a full re-roof the property needed anyway — insurers notice this and it can lead to claim denial. Equally, don't minimise the claim — if there is water ingress damage to ceilings and decorations as well as the roof covering, include it all.

Understand your excess. Most home insurance policies have an excess — a fixed amount you pay before the insurer covers the rest. Make sure you know yours before deciding whether to claim — a small repair that costs less than your excess is better handled directly without involving the insurer (and avoiding the potential effect on your premium at renewal).

Emergency repairs vs full repairs. Insurers understand that emergency weatherproofing needs to happen immediately to prevent further damage, even before a loss adjuster has visited. Keep receipts and records of any emergency spend. The full repair claim can then follow the insurer's assessment process.

Protecting yourself from rogue traders after storms

This section matters enormously and we make no apology for addressing it directly. After every significant storm across East Sussex, rogue roofing traders appear in large numbers. They drive slowly through residential streets looking for visible roof damage, knock on doors, and claim they've "just noticed" a problem — offering to fix it immediately, usually for cash.

These individuals cause significant harm to East Sussex homeowners every year. The "work" they carry out — if they carry out any — is typically substandard, uses poor materials, and in many cases makes the original problem worse. Customers pay substantial cash sums and discover weeks later that the roof still leaks or that work described as completed was never actually done.

How to spot a rogue trader:

  • They knock on your door cold, claiming to have "noticed" damage you hadn't seen
  • They create urgency — "we're working in the street today, we can do it now"
  • They want cash payment, often large amounts upfront
  • They don't provide a written quote before starting work
  • They can't provide a verifiable local address or landline number
  • They're unwilling to provide insurance details
  • They pressure you to decide before you've had a chance to check them out

How to reduce storm damage risk on your East Sussex roof

You can't control the weather, but you can significantly reduce the risk that a storm will cause serious damage to your roof by keeping it in good condition. The roofs that suffer worst in East Sussex storms are almost always those with pre-existing problems — loose ridge tiles that needed repointing, slates held by corroded nails, gutters already blocked with moss and debris.

  • Have the roof inspected every 3–5 years — catching loose ridge tiles and failing fixings before a storm is far cheaper than emergency repairs after one
  • Keep gutters clear — full gutters add weight and wind resistance, making them more likely to fail in a storm
  • Address ridge tile mortar before it fails — once mortar begins to crack, the next storm will finish the job
  • Consider mechanically fixed ridge tiles on exposed properties in Polegate, Pevensey Bay, Bexhill seafront and other high-wind areas
  • Treat moss promptly — heavy moss growth adds load and holds moisture that accelerates mortar and fixing deterioration
  • Keep trees near the property maintained — overhanging branches are the cause of a significant proportion of roof damage in East Sussex's autumn and winter storms

Questions & answers — storm damage in East Sussex

The storm was last week — how quickly do I need to act?

As quickly as possible. Every day of rain entering through a gap in your roof covering is adding to the water damage inside the property and the structural moisture load on your roof timbers. If you've noticed missing tiles or any sign of water ingress, call us today. We can apply emergency weatherproofing very quickly if needed.

Will my insurance cover storm damage?

Standard home buildings insurance covers sudden storm damage to a roof that was in reasonable condition before the storm. It doesn't cover gradual deterioration or wear and tear. Contact your insurer promptly, document everything before repairs begin, and ask us for a written storm damage assessment to support your claim.

Someone knocked on my door and said my roof is damaged — should I let them fix it?

No. Cold-calling rogue traders are a serious problem in East Sussex after every major storm. Decline politely, and then check your roof yourself from the ground with binoculars, or call an established local roofer like us for a free inspection. We can tell you what's actually damaged and what it will cost to fix honestly.
